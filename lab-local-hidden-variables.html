Lab · Local Hidden Variables

Can nature be explained by local hidden variables?

In 1935, Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen argued that quantum mechanics must be incomplete. Two particles, once entangled, appear to share correlated outcomes no matter how far apart they travel. To Einstein, this "spooky action at a distance" was evidence that each particle must carry a set of pre-existing instructions — a local hidden variable — that determined its answer before the measurement ever happened. No spookiness. Just classical correlation, like two sealed envelopes.

For thirty years this was a matter of taste. Then in 1964, John Bell proved that the two pictures make different numerical predictions — predictions you can measure. In this lab, you will reproduce his experiment. You choose the source, the detector angles, the number of trials. The data decides.

"It is a matter of indifference… whether the objects of physics are the elements of a complete or an incomplete description." — after Bell, whose inequality closed the argument.

The apparatus

  • A central source emits pairs of particles, one to Alice, one to Bob.
  • Alice independently chooses one of two settings: a₀ or a₁.
  • Bob independently chooses one of two settings: b₀ or b₁.
  • Each detector returns exactly one binary outcome: +1 or −1.
  • Alice and Bob cannot signal each other during a trial.

Your role

  • You are the experimentalist. You do not know in advance whether the source is classical or quantum.
  • You choose angles, trial counts, and whether to add detector noise.
  • You observe four correlators E(aᵢ, bⱼ) and combine them into the CHSH statistic S.
  • Your job: decide from the data whether a local hidden-variable model of this kind can explain what you measured.

Task 02

Combine the correlators into a test statistic.

Bell showed that any theory where each particle carries local, pre-existing instructions — no matter how elaborate — must satisfy a specific numerical bound on the combination

S = E(a₀, b₀) − E(a₀, b₁) + E(a₁, b₀) + E(a₁, b₁)

The bound is |S| ≤ 2. Quantum mechanics, with an entangled source and well-chosen angles, predicts a maximum of |S| = 2√2 ≈ 2.828. Anything above 2 is a problem for any local hidden-variable model. Your measured value, with error bars from finite statistics, is below.

Task 03

Compare classical and quantum predictions across angle differences.

A single CHSH number is a summary. To see where the two theories diverge, plot the correlator E(θ) as a function of the angle difference θ = |α − β| between Alice's and Bob's detectors. The best local hidden-variable model achieves a linear correlation curve; the quantum prediction is +cos(θ). They touch at 0° and 180° — and separate in between.

The curve below shows both predictions overlaid with the four points you measured. The gap between the curves at 22.5° and 67.5° is exactly what pushes CHSH past the classical bound.
