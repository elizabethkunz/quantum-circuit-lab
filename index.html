A note on noise

Explore the noise model.

Real quantum computers are not the idealized machines these diagrams imply. Every physical gate is a short pulse of laser light or microwave — a tiny, error-prone operation. Over a circuit's lifetime, those errors pile up and smear clean results into a murky distribution.

The slider simulates depolarizing noise, the simplest useful error model: after each gate, with some probability, a random Pauli error (X, Y, or Z) is applied in place of nothing. Load the Bell pair from Templates, set noise to 2%, and run — you'll see the forbidden outcomes |01⟩ and |10⟩ quietly appear. That gap between the ideal and the noisy is what quantum error correction is trying to close.

Gate glossary

H — Hadamard

Applies the unitary \( H=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{bmatrix} \), mapping computational basis states to X-basis states: \( |0\rangle \to |+\rangle \) and \( |1\rangle \to |-\rangle \). It is self-inverse (\( H^2=I \)) and converts phase information into measurable population differences, which is why it appears before and after many oracle/query blocks.

X — Bit flip (Pauli-X)

The Pauli-X operator swaps amplitudes of \( |0\rangle \) and \( |1\rangle \), acting as a \( \pi \) rotation about the Bloch x-axis: \( X=\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{bmatrix} \). On basis states it behaves like a classical NOT, but on superpositions it changes relative amplitudes coherently rather than "flipping a stored bit."

Z, S, T — Phase gates

Diagonal unitaries that preserve populations in the Z basis while changing relative phase: \( Z=\mathrm{diag}(1,-1) \), \( S=\mathrm{diag}(1,i) \), \( T=\mathrm{diag}(1,e^{i\pi/4}) \). Their effect is often invisible in immediate measurement statistics, but they reshape later interference and are essential for universal fault-tolerant gate synthesis.

Rₓ, R_y, R_z — Rotation gates

Continuous one-qubit rotations \( R_n(\theta)=e^{-i\theta\sigma_n/2} \) for \( n\in\{x,y,z\} \). They move the Bloch vector by angle \( \theta \) around the chosen axis, with X/Y/Z recovered at \( \theta=\pi \) (up to global phase). Parameterized rotations are the core trainable primitives in VQE, QAOA, and hardware-efficient ansatz circuits.

CNOT — Controlled NOT

A two-qubit entangling gate that applies \( X \) to the target conditioned on \( \mathrm{control}=|1\rangle \). Matrix-wise, it is block-diagonal with \( I \) on the control-\( |0\rangle \) subspace and \( X \) on control-\( |1\rangle \). Acting on superposed controls, it correlates qubits nonclassically and is the standard primitive for Bell-state generation and many algorithmic oracles.

◎ Measure

Projective readout in the computational basis. A qubit state α|0⟩+β|1⟩ yields 0 with probability |α|² and 1 with probability |β|², and the post-measurement state collapses to the observed eigenstate. This non-unitary step irreversibly converts quantum amplitude information into classical data.

Entanglement: two qubits, one fate.

Now add a CNOT between the two qubits, right after the first Hadamard. The circuit is suddenly doing something no classical system can. Run it — only 00 and 11 appear, never 01 or 10. The two qubits always agree, even though each one individually is random.

Step 02

The CNOT gate: conditional logic, quantum consequences.

The CNOT (controlled-NOT) gate takes two qubits: a control and a target. The rule: flip the target if and only if the control is |1⟩. On classical definite states, it's just conditional logic. But when the control is in superposition, something stranger happens — and a second surprise lurks when the target is in a special state.

Classical analogue — reversible XOR
Bits: (c, t) ↦ (c, c ⊕ t)

On classical bits, the reversible XOR (or “controlled-NOT”) copies the control forward and XORs it into the target: the target flips exactly when c = 1, and the control line is unchanged. That is the same input–output pattern as the CNOT on |0⟩ and |1⟩.

Qubits: unitary CNOT

The quantum CNOT is the same linear map on the computational basis, but it is unitary: it accepts superpositions and can create entanglement (Part B) and move phase between branches (Part C) — effects that have no single-bit classical XOR story.

Step 05

What you've learned about entanglement.

Two-qubit state space is 4-dimensional, not 2+2. Product states can be described by their parts; entangled states cannot. The CNOT gate creates entanglement when its control is in superposition. Measuring one qubit updates your description of the joint state and therefore the conditional prediction for the partner outcome; it does not transmit a usable superluminal message.

These aren't philosophical curiosities. Entanglement is the resource that gives quantum computers and networks their power. The density matrix tutorial (next) shows how to distinguish genuine superposition from classical randomness — a distinction that matters enormously for quantum hardware.

Step 02

Populations and coherences — decoherence in time.

For one qubit, the density matrix \( \rho \) has diagonal entries \( \rho_{00} \), \( \rho_{11} \) called populations — they set the probabilities for outcomes 0 and 1 in a \( Z \) measurement. The off-diagonals \( \rho_{01}=\rho_{10}^* \) are coherences — they encode quantum phase between \( |0\rangle \) and \( |1\rangle \). Many noise models (especially pure dephasing, tied to \( T_2 \)) shrink coherences while leaving populations fixed, so a state can slide from a pure superposition toward a classical mixture without changing the 50/50 \( Z \) odds.

Step 03

\( \rho \) for classical ignorance: statistical mixtures.

This step is only about classical randomness: you prepare \( |0\rangle \) or \( |1\rangle \) according to a coin flip (or any probabilities \( p \), \( 1-p \)). There is no superposition in each run — you simply don’t know which ket you got. Von Neumann’s density matrix packages that ignorance into one object:

\( \displaystyle \rho \;=\; \sum_i p_i\, |\psi_i\rangle\langle\psi_i| \;=\; p\,|0\rangle\langle 0| + (1-p)\,|1\rangle\langle 1| \).

Here every \( |\psi_i\rangle\langle\psi_i| \) is a pure-state projector (rank 1). The sum is not a ket: it is a weighted average of projectors. For this ensemble, \( \rho \) is always diagonal in the \( Z \) basis — coherences are identically zero because there is no phase relation between branches you are classically mixing.

Step 04

Same \( \rho \), richer states — pure, correlated, entangled.

The same symbol \( \rho \) describes more than classical mixtures. A pure state \( |\psi\rangle \) has \( \rho = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi| \): a single projector — rank 1, off-diagonals allowed, encoding superposition and phase. That is not the same as averaging \( |0\rangle\langle 0| \) and \( |1\rangle\langle 1| \): Step 3’s \( \rho \) was built from several orthogonal projectors with classical weights; a pure \( |+\rangle \) is one projector onto a superposed direction, with \( \rho_{01}\neq 0 \).

For several qubits, \( \rho \) is a larger positive matrix. If the parties are uncorrelated, a product state has \( \rho = \rho_A \otimes \rho_B \) (tensor product). Entangled states (Tutorial 2) still have a density matrix — but it is not a product: for example the Bell state \( |\Phi^+\rangle \) has a \( 4\times 4 \) matrix with off-diagonal terms linking \( |00\rangle\langle 11| \) and \( |11\rangle\langle 00| \). You cannot reconstruct that from separate one-qubit \( \rho_A \) and \( \rho_B \) alone; the joint \( \rho \) carries the correlation. So: mixture (Step 3) = classical weights on known pure states; general \( \rho \) = full quantum state, including superposition and multi-qubit entanglement.

The panel below is back to one qubit: interpolate from pure \( |+\rangle \) to the same-population maximally mixed state as in Step 3 at \( p=\tfrac12 \). Only coherences change — the diagonal stays \( \tfrac12,\tfrac12 \). That is how the same formalism distinguishes quantum coherence from classical mixing.

Step 06

Purity on the Bloch ball — length of the state vector.

For a single qubit, any \( \rho \) can be pictured as a point inside the Bloch ball: a vector \( \vec r \) from the origin with \( |\vec r|\le 1 \). Pure states sit on the surface (\( |\vec r|=1 \)). Mixed states sit inside; the maximally mixed state \( I/2 \) is \( \vec r=\vec 0 \).

The purity \( P = \mathrm{Tr}(\rho^2) \) is a single number (1 for pure, \( \tfrac12 \) for a qubit maximally mixed). For one qubit it ties directly to the Bloch length: \( P = (1 + |\vec r|^2)/2 \), i.e. \( |\vec r| = \sqrt{2P - 1} \). Step 07

What you now understand about quantum state description.

Pure states and mixed states both exist in quantum mechanics. They can produce identical Z-basis statistics but differ in every other basis — the difference is entirely in the off-diagonal elements of ρ. The density matrix is the complete description of any quantum state, pure or mixed.

Decoherence is the physical process by which off-diagonals decay. When a qubit interacts with its environment, the environment effectively "measures" it continuously — the Bloch vector shrinks from the surface toward the centre, and the quantum advantage erodes. Fighting this is the entire engineering challenge of quantum computing.

Step 02

Why you can't just copy.

Classical computers handle noise by redundancy. If a hard drive bit might flip, store it three times: 000. Later, check all three — majority rules.

Quantum computers can't do this directly. The no-cloning theorem says there is no machine that, given an unknown qubit, produces an independent copy of it. The key point is linearity/unitarity: if a machine cloned basis states, applying it to a superposition would violate linear evolution because \(U(\alpha|\psi_1\rangle+\beta|\psi_2\rangle)|0\rangle\) cannot equal \((\alpha|\psi_1\rangle+\beta|\psi_2\rangle)\otimes(\alpha|\psi_1\rangle+\beta|\psi_2\rangle)\). The theorem was proved in 1982 and is completely airtight.[E1]

Why linearity blocks copying

A would-be cloner can duplicate known basis states, but a superposition is not a mixture of the two pictures — the joint output is not a tensor of two |+⟩ states.

Implication

Quantum error correction cannot copy a qubit. It has to protect it some other way — by entangling it with helper qubits so the information is spread across many physical qubits that can be measured safely.

Step 04

The surface code: stabilizers on a sheet.

The three-qubit repetition idea only protects against bit flips (Pauli X) along a single copy of the line. Real hardware also suffers phase flips (Pauli Z) and mixed noise. A practical code has to detect many error types without learning the logical information you are trying to protect.

The surface code places physical qubits on a two-dimensional lattice. On each small face (plaquette) of the grid, the code repeatedly measures stabilizer operators — multi-qubit parity checks whose outcomes should be trivial in the absence of noise. An error flips some of those outcomes, producing a syndrome: a pattern of violated checks that points to where something went wrong. A decoder turns that pattern into recovery operations. The distance d (roughly the width of the patch) sets how many independent failures you can correct before a logical error gets through.

Unlike the toy line code, the surface code is a leading candidate for real devices because its checks are local on a grid and its behavior is well studied — at a cost: protecting one logical qubit takes on the order of d² physical qubits. Step 06

Too many errors: when correction breaks down.

A distance-d code is built to handle a limited number of faults per error-correction cycle. In the d = 3 surface patch you explored, a single data-qubit bit flip changes the syndromes in a way a decoder can usually fix. If two or more independent bit flips land in the same round, the pattern can be ambiguous: recovery might guess wrong, or the noise is simply beyond what that patch was designed to correct in one shot. As physical error rate grows, multi-error events become common — the logical qubit is overwhelmed.

Threshold theorem (intuition): there is a crossover physical error rate pth (often order 1% for the surface code in good models). Below pth, making the code larger (bigger d) makes the logical error rate drop steeply. Above pth, noise is so high that error correction and the extra gates it needs can add more damage than they remove — in practice you do not get arbitrarily reliable logical qubits by scaling the code. Real fault tolerance requires staying under threshold and good decoding and implementation.

What you now know.

You know that quantum computers can't use classical redundancy because of the no-cloning theorem. You know that information can instead be spread across many physical qubits by entanglement. The problem: one query or two?

Imagine a black-box function f that takes an n-bit string and outputs 0 or 1. You're promised it is either constant (outputs the same value for every input) or balanced (outputs 0 for exactly half the inputs and 1 for the rest). How many queries does it take to decide which?

Classically, you might need to evaluate f on up to 2n−1+1 inputs — more than half the input space — before you can be certain. Quantum mechanics does it in exactly one query, regardless of n. This is the Deutsch–Jozsa (D-J) algorithm, the first proof of quantum computational advantage.[E1]

Step 02

The circuit: Hadamards, an oracle, more Hadamards.

The D-J algorithm uses n+1 qubits. The top n qubits start as |0⟩ and the bottom ancilla starts as |1⟩. The key steps:

  1. Apply H to all n+1 qubits → uniform superposition + ancilla in |−⟩
  2. Apply the oracle Uf (one query, encodes f as a phase)
  3. Apply H to the top n qubits
  4. Measure the top n qubits — all 0s means constant, any 1 means balanced

The magic is phase kickback (you saw it in Tutorial 2): the oracle flips the ancilla's phase onto the input register, encoding the function's structure in interference patterns. The final H layer then constructively interferes the inputs that "agree" and destructively cancels the rest.

Step 06

What D-J taught the field.

Deutsch–Jozsa was the first demonstration that quantum computers can solve a specific problem with exponentially fewer queries than any classical algorithm. The speedup is not about raw speed — it's about the number of times you need to evaluate the oracle.

The key ideas you've now seen: phase kickback encodes the oracle's output into qubit phases. A global superposition queries all inputs at once. Interference combines the results — the Hadamards at the end constructively amplify the answer and cancel everything else.

D-J is a toy problem — no one actually cares whether mystery functions are constant or balanced. But the same circuit structure, scaled up, powers phase estimation (which is inside Shor's algorithm) and Grover's search. The idiom of "superpose → oracle → interfere → measure" runs through the whole field.

Historical note

Step 07

What Grover taught the field.

Grover showed that unstructured search is one of the cleanest places where quantum mechanics gives a provable asymptotic advantage: from O(N) classical queries down to O(√N) oracle calls.

The mechanism is geometric: repeated oracle-plus-diffusion steps perform a controlled rotation of the state vector toward the marked subspace. The gain comes from interference and phase structure, not from reading all answers at once.

Just as importantly, Grover teaches limits. The speedup is quadratic, not exponential, and practical performance is sensitive to noise and to choosing the right number of iterations before overshoot.

Historical note

Lov Grover published the algorithm in 1996, and it became a cornerstone result in quantum complexity theory. It is still the canonical baseline for "black-box" search and appears as a subroutine in many larger quantum algorithms.

Why cats in a bosonic mode?

Many physical platforms store quantum information in a harmonic oscillator — a single electromagnetic mode in a mirror or a superconducting cavity, or an optical pulse sent from Alice to Bob in continuous-variable (CV) protocols. A fourth challenge, not listed here but arguably the most fundamental, is the barren plateau problem: for sufficiently deep parameterized circuits, the gradient of the cost function with respect to any parameter becomes exponentially small in the number of qubits. This means classical optimizers have essentially no useful signal to follow — the landscape becomes flat everywhere. Barren plateaus are a known theoretical obstacle to scaling VQE beyond small systems, and resolving them is an active research area. The literature

This page includes a timeline of the most consequential papers in quantum circuits, and then general references cited throughout this site.

Landmark papers
1964
Bell — On the Einstein Podolsky Rosen Paradox
›
J. S. Bell · Physics 1, 195 (1964)

Proves that no local hidden-variable theory can reproduce all predictions of quantum mechanics. The inequality that bears his name separates quantum correlations from any classical probabilistic explanation. Every quantum networking and cryptography protocol in existence depends on the physical reality this paper established. The CHSH test in Tutorial 2 Step 4 is a direct implementation of Bell's argument, and the 2022 Nobel Prize in Physics was awarded for its experimental confirmation.

Read paper →
1985
Deutsch — Quantum Theory, the Church–Turing Principle and the Universal Quantum Computer
›
D. Deutsch · Proc. R. Soc. Lond. A 400 (1985)

The founding paper of quantum computation. Deutsch formalises the quantum Turing machine and introduces the first quantum algorithm — a single-bit problem (is this function constant or balanced?) solved in one oracle query where any classical algorithm needs two. The circuit idiom of "prepare superposition → query oracle → interfere → measure" originates here, and every algorithm in this site's Templates section is a descendant. Tutorial 5 explores the n-bit generalisation of this paper's core circuit.

Read paper →
1992
Deutsch & Jozsa — Rapid Solution of Problems by Quantum Computation
›
D. Deutsch, R. Jozsa · Proc. R. Soc. Lond. A 439 (1992)

Extends Deutsch's algorithm to n input bits: determine whether a function is constant or balanced using a single oracle query, versus the 2n−1+1 queries needed classically in the worst case. The circuit built and studied in Tutorial 5 is exactly the one in this paper. It introduces phase kickback as an explicit mechanism — the ancilla qubit in state |−⟩ absorbs the oracle's action as a phase on the input register, which the final Hadamard layer then converts to a readable measurement result.

Read paper →
1994
Shor — Algorithms for Quantum Computation: Discrete Logarithms and Factoring
›
P. W. Shor · FOCS 1994 / SIAM J. Comput. 26 (1997)

Exponential speedup for integer factorisation and discrete logarithm — the paper that made governments pay attention to quantum computing. At its core is quantum phase estimation, which uses the same Hadamard–oracle–Hadamard structure as Deutsch–Jozsa, combined with a quantum Fourier transform. If a large fault-tolerant machine is ever built at scale, RSA encryption becomes obsolete. The gate set used in this site's Lab (H, CNOT, Rz, T, S) is exactly what Shor's algorithm requires.

Read paper →
1995
Shor — Scheme for Reducing Decoherence in Quantum Computer Memory
›
P. W. Shor · Phys. Rev. A 52, R2493 (1995)

The first quantum error-correcting code: one logical qubit encoded in nine physical qubits, resistant to arbitrary single-qubit errors. Proves that fault-tolerant quantum computation is not in principle impossible. The mechanism — measuring syndrome qubits without disturbing the encoded state — is the same mechanism demonstrated in Tutorial 4's bit-flip encoder and conceptually extended in the surface code grid. The repetition code in Tutorial 4 Step 3 is a simplified version of one building block of this paper.

Read paper →
2000
Nielsen & Chuang — Quantum Computation and Quantum Information
›
M. A. Nielsen, I. L. Chuang · Cambridge University Press (2000)

The standard textbook. Universally known as "Mike and Ike." Every gate definition, density matrix convention, Bloch sphere notation, and noise model in this site follows Nielsen & Chuang's conventions. Chapter 2 (linear algebra and qubits), Chapter 4 (quantum circuits), Chapter 8 (noise and decoherence), and Chapter 10 (quantum error correction) are the most directly relevant sections for understanding what the tutorials and simulator are doing. If any notation here seems unfamiliar, this book will resolve it.

View / buy →
2019
Google Quantum AI — Quantum supremacy using a programmable superconducting processor
›
Arute et al. · Nature 574, 505–510 (2019)

First claimed quantum computational advantage: a 53-qubit Sycamore processor runs a random circuit sampling task in 200 seconds, estimated to take 10,000 years on the best classical computers available at the time (though subsequent classical advances have narrowed that gap). The gate set — Hadamard, CNOT, single-qubit rotations — is exactly the palette in this site's Lab. The depolarizing noise model used throughout this site is a simplified version of the per-gate error model characterised and reported in this experiment.
