A note on noise

Try the noise slider.

Real quantum computers are not the idealized machines these diagrams imply. Every physical gate is a short pulse of laser light or microwave — a tiny, error-prone operation. Over a circuit's lifetime, those errors pile up and smear clean results into a murky distribution.

The slider simulates depolarizing noise, the simplest useful error model: after each gate, with some probability, a random Pauli error (X, Y, or Z) is applied in place of nothing. Load the Bell pair from Templates, set noise to 2%, and run — you'll see the forbidden outcomes |01⟩ and |10⟩ quietly appear. That gap between the ideal and the noisy is what quantum error correction is trying to close.

Gate glossary

H — Hadamard
Turns a definite state into an equal superposition. Press it twice and you're back where you started. The engine of quantum parallelism.
X — Bit flip
The quantum NOT gate. 0 becomes 1, 1 becomes 0. Behaves like its classical cousin on definite states.
Z, S, T — Phase gates
Invisible on their own (they don't change measurement probabilities), but they rotate how qubits interfere with each other later. Essential for real algorithms.
CNOT — Controlled NOT
Flips the target qubit only if the control qubit is 1. Combined with H, this is how entanglement is built.
◎ Measure
Forces a qubit to commit to a definite 0 or 1. Irreversible. Ends the quantum part of the computation.

Step 02

A qubit lives on a sphere.

A qubit is the quantum analogue of a bit — but its state isn't just 0 or 1. It's any point on the surface of a sphere, called the Bloch sphere. The north pole is |0⟩. The south pole is |1⟩. Everything in between is a superposition — a genuine blend of the two.

Drag the arrow below, or click one of the preset states. Notice that points around the equator are equal superpositions of |0⟩ and |1⟩ — they differ only in their phase, which matters later but is invisible to a simple measurement.

Step 03

Gates are rotations.

On the Bloch sphere, every single-qubit gate is a rotation. The X gate rotates 180° around the x-axis — it flips |0⟩ to |1⟩. The Z gate rotates 180° around the z-axis. The H (Hadamard) gate takes |0⟩ to |+⟩ — halfway to the equator, the birthplace of superposition.

Reset the qubit to |0⟩, then press gates in different orders. Gate order matters — XH is not the same as HX. This is one of the clearest ways quantum differs from classical logic.

Step 04

Measurement destroys superposition.

On the sphere, a qubit can be anywhere. But when you measure it, you only ever get a classical answer: 0 or 1. The probability of each is set by the arrow's position — specifically, how far it leans toward the north or south pole.

Step 05

Two qubits, four possibilities.

Two classical bits have four states: 00, 01, 10, 11. Two qubits also have four basis states — but a qubit system can be in a weighted combination of all four at once. That's the real reason quantum computers can explore large state spaces so efficiently.

The circuit below puts Hadamard on both qubits. The result: all four outcomes appear with equal probability. This is a product state — each qubit is independent. Run it and see.

Step 06

Entanglement: two qubits, one fate.

Now add a CNOT between the two qubits, right after the first Hadamard. The circuit is suddenly doing something no classical system can. Run it — only 00 and 11 appear, never 01 or 10. The two qubits always agree, even though each one individually is random.

This is entanglement. Not "correlated" in the everyday sense — linked in a way that can't be reproduced by hidden prior agreement. Bell's theorem, proven in the 1960s and experimentally confirmed many times (most recently earning a Nobel prize in 2022), shows this is a genuinely new kind of relationship.

Real machines make mistakes.

Everything above has assumed perfect gates. Real quantum computers are short pulses of laser light, microwaves, or magnetic fields, and every pulse is slightly imperfect. Over a circuit's lifetime, tiny errors accumulate and the clean peaks smear.

Step 08

You have the vocabulary.

You've met the core ideas: superposition (a qubit in between), gates (rotations on the sphere), measurement (the collapse to a classical bit), entanglement (qubits sharing a fate), and noise (why it's all so hard).

Everything else in quantum computing — teleportation, Grover's search, Shor's factoring, phase estimation, error correction — is built out of these five. The Lab has a playground where you can drag gates and try things yourself. Tutorial 2 explains how error correction works its way around the noise problem.

Step 04

Measuring one qubit collapses the other.

In a Bell pair, neither qubit has a definite value — but they're correlated. When you measure q0, two things happen at once: q0 becomes a definite 0 or 1, and q1 instantly settles to the same value, no matter how far apart they are. This is what Einstein called "spooky action at a distance."

Step 05

What you've learned about entanglement.

Two-qubit state space is 4-dimensional, not 2+2. Product states can be described by their parts; entangled states cannot. The CNOT gate creates entanglement when its control is in superposition. Measuring one qubit of an entangled pair collapses the other — instantaneously, always, perfectly.

These aren't philosophical curiosities. Entanglement is the resource that gives quantum computers and networks their power. The density matrix tutorial (next) shows how to distinguish genuine superposition from classical randomness — a distinction that matters enormously for quantum hardware.

Step 02

The density matrix: one object to rule them all.

A pure state can be written as a vector |ψ⟩. A mixed state cannot — it's a probability distribution over pure states. The density matrix ρ handles both cases in one framework: for a pure state, ρ = |ψ⟩⟨ψ|. For a mixture of states with probabilities pᵢ, ρ = Σ pᵢ |ψᵢ⟩⟨ψᵢ|.

The 2×2 matrices below update live as you move the slider. Watch what changes — and what doesn't — as you blend from pure superposition to the classical mixture. The diagonal entries (called populations) tell you measurement probabilities. The off-diagonal entries (called coherences) encode the quantum interference that pure states have and mixtures don't.

Step 03

Off-diagonals are physically real.

The off-diagonal entry ρ₀₁ isn't just a mathematical object — it's physically measurable. It's the amplitude for interference between the |0⟩ and |1⟩ components. When you measure in the X-basis (apply H, then measure), you're directly probing this interference:

  • Pure |+⟩: ρ₀₁ = 0.5 — X-basis always gives 0
  • Classical mixture: ρ₀₁ = 0 — X-basis gives 50/50 random

Select a state and run measurements in both bases. The X-basis result directly tells you whether coherence is present — you can't fake it with classical probability.

Step 04

Mixed states live inside the sphere.

In Tutorial 1, the Bloch sphere showed pure states as points on the surface. But mixed states aren't pure — they're convex combinations of pure states, and their Bloch vector has length less than 1. The maximally mixed state ρ = I/2 sits exactly at the center.

Drag the decoherence slider and watch the Bloch vector shrink from the surface toward the origin. The length of the vector is exactly √(Tr(ρ²) × 2 − 1) — a direct measure of purity. When hardware engineers say "T₂ time" (coherence time), they're measuring how long before their qubit's Bloch vector reaches the center.

Step 05

What you now understand about quantum state description.

Pure states and mixed states both exist in quantum mechanics. They can produce identical Z-basis statistics but differ in every other basis — the difference is entirely in the off-diagonal elements of ρ. The density matrix is the complete description of any quantum state, pure or mixed.

Decoherence is the physical process by which off-diagonals decay. When a qubit interacts with its environment, the environment effectively "measures" it continuously — the Bloch vector shrinks from the surface toward the centre, and the quantum advantage erodes. Fighting this is the entire engineering challenge of quantum computing.

Step 02

Why you can't just copy.

Classical computers handle noise by redundancy. If a hard drive bit might flip, store it three times: 000. Later, check all three — majority rules.

Quantum computers can't do this directly. The no-cloning theorem says there is no machine that, given an unknown qubit, produces an independent copy of it. This is a consequence of how quantum gates work — they're reversible linear operations, and a cloning operation would not be linear. The theorem was proved in 1982 and is completely airtight.

Implication

Quantum error correction cannot copy a qubit. It has to protect it some other way — by entangling it with helper qubits so the information is spread across many physical qubits that can be measured safely.

Read and continue.

Step 04

Surface codes and the overhead problem.

Real error correction uses codes like the surface code, where a grid of physical qubits protects a single "logical qubit" against both bit flips and phase flips. Roughly speaking, each extra unit of protection doubles the reliability — but also multiplies the physical qubit count.

Current estimates for running algorithms like Shor's on 2048-bit RSA suggest you'd need millions of physical qubits protecting a few thousand logical ones. Today's largest machines have a few hundred physical qubits. The gap between where we are and where we need to be is mostly a matter of quality, not raw quantity.

The threshold theorem

If the error rate per gate is below a certain threshold (roughly 1%, give or take depending on the code), then you can use enough redundancy to drive the logical error rate arbitrarily low. Machines today are just starting to cross this threshold. That's why this is the decade when things start to get interesting.

Almost there.
Step 05

What you now know.

You know that quantum computers can't use classical redundancy because of the no-cloning theorem. You know that information can instead be spread across many physical qubits by entanglement. You know that real codes like the surface code add substantial overhead — and that the threshold theorem promises this is worth it once gates are good enough.

This is real, current frontier-of-the-field material. Every major quantum company — IBM, Google, Quantinuum, PsiQuantum, IonQ — is racing not for more qubits, but for better ones. The ones that cross the threshold first will run the first useful algorithms.

The literature, briefly annotated.

The canonical papers, standard textbooks, and a few key online resources. Organised by theme — start anywhere.

Foundations

Feynman · 1982
Simulating Physics with Computers

The paper that started it all. Feynman argues that classical computers cannot efficiently simulate quantum systems, and that a quantum computer could. The seed of the entire field.

foundationalsimulation
Deutsch · 1985
Quantum Theory, the Church-Turing Principle and the Universal Quantum Computer

Formalises the quantum Turing machine and presents the first quantum algorithm — a simple problem solved in one query where classical computers need two.

foundationalalgorithms
Bell · 1964
On the Einstein Podolsky Rosen Paradox

Bell proves that no local hidden-variable theory can reproduce all predictions of quantum mechanics. Entanglement is not just a bookkeeping trick — it's physically real.

entanglementnonlocality
Wootters & Zurek · 1982
A Single Quantum Cannot Be Cloned

The no-cloning theorem, proved in one page. An unknown quantum state cannot be duplicated — with direct consequences for error correction, cryptography, and quantum networking.

no-cloningtheorem

Algorithms

Shor · 1994
Algorithms for Quantum Computation: Discrete Logarithms and Factoring

Exponential speedup for integer factorisation. If a fault-tolerant quantum computer is ever built at scale, RSA encryption becomes obsolete. The paper that made governments pay attention.

factoringcryptographyexponential speedup
Grover · 1996
A Fast Quantum Mechanical Algorithm for Database Search

Quadratic speedup for unstructured search: finds an item in N possibilities using only √N queries. The canonical example of amplitude amplification, now a standard subroutine in larger algorithms.

searchquadratic speedup
Harrow, Hassidim & Lloyd · 2009
Quantum Algorithm for Linear Systems of Equations (HHL)

Exponential speedup for solving sparse linear systems under certain conditions. Foundational for proposed quantum speedups in machine learning and optimisation, though the caveats are steep.

linear algebrahhl

Error correction

Shor · 1995
Scheme for Reducing Decoherence in Quantum Computer Memory

The first quantum error-correcting code. Encodes one logical qubit in nine physical qubits and corrects arbitrary single-qubit errors — proving fault-tolerant quantum computing is not in principle impossible.

Kitaev · 2003
Fault-Tolerant Quantum Computation by Anyons

Introduces the toric code and topological quantum error correction. The surface code — used by Google and IBM — is its planar variant. The idea: encode information in global topology, not local physical bits.

Fowler et al. · 2012
Surface Codes: Towards Practical Large-Scale Quantum Computation

The definitive reference for the surface code. Covers thresholds, resource estimates, and logical gate sets. If you want to understand what IBM and Google are actually building, start here.
