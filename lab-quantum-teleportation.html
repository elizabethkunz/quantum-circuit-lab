Can you send a quantum state using only classical bits?

Quantum states cannot be copied — the no-cloning theorem forbids it. So how does a quantum computer move a qubit from one location to another? In 1993, Bennett, Brassard, Crépeau, Jozsa, Peres, and Wootters showed the answer: consume a pre-shared entangled pair as a resource, make two classical measurements, and send those two bits across an ordinary channel. The receiver applies one of four simple corrections, and the original quantum state materialises — perfectly, with no physical particle ever moving between them.

This is quantum teleportation. Not faster-than-light — the two classical bits must travel normally. But it is the only known way to faithfully move a qubit across space.

"Quantum teleportation combines entanglement and classical communication to achieve what is impossible for either alone." — after Bennett et al. 1993, the paper that proved it.

The cast

  • Alice holds one qubit she wants to send (the payload), plus one half of a pre-shared Bell pair.
  • Bob holds the other half of the Bell pair, separated from Alice.
  • Alice makes two measurements, gets two classical bits, and sends them to Bob.
  • Bob applies a Pauli correction based on those bits. The payload state appears on his qubit.

The rules

  • Alice's original payload is destroyed by her measurement — no cloning occurs.
  • Bob's qubit is random until he receives the two classical bits. No FTL signalling.
  • Fidelity is exactly 1.0 with no noise. Noise degrades this — quantifiably.
  • The classical bits carry no information about the payload state itself.

Task 04

What noise does to teleportation fidelity.

Perfect teleportation requires a perfect Bell pair and perfect gates. Two distinct noise channels degrade fidelity: gate depolarising noise affects the preparation of the Bell pair and Alice's operations; classical channel noise flips the transmitted bits with some probability. The two sources degrade fidelity in different ways.

Fidelity under gate noise degrades smoothly: each imperfect gate adds a small error. Channel noise is more abrupt — a single bit-flip on Alice's classical bits causes Bob to apply the wrong correction, and the state is completely scrambled for that trial. At 50% channel noise both bits are effectively random and fidelity drops to the classical limit of 2/3 (the best possible with no entanglement at all).

You have run quantum teleportation from first principles.

Work through the findings below — each one is something you observed directly, not something you were told.

Task 01 · The protocol
Regardless of the payload state — |0⟩, |+⟩, |+i⟩ or anything else — stage ⑥ always reproduces it on Bob's qubit. The circuit structure guarantees this, not the measurement outcome.
Task 02 · Perfect fidelity
With zero noise, F = 100% exactly. This is not a statistical artifact — it is a consequence of the unitarity of the gates and the structure of the Bell pair.
Task 03 · Uniform bits
All four correction cases (00, 01, 10, 11) occur with equal probability regardless of payload. Alice's bits are maximally random — an eavesdropper who intercepts them learns nothing about the state being teleported.
Task 04 · Two kinds of noise
Gate depolarising noise degrades fidelity smoothly across every trial. Classical channel noise corrupts whole trials abruptly when a bit flips. The two sources compound independently and must be controlled separately.

Quantum teleportation is the primitive behind quantum networking, quantum repeaters, and measurement-based quantum computing. Everything you explored here scales directly to those applications.
