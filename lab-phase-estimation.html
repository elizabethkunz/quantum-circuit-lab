Shor's subroutine:
Quantum Phase Estimation

In 1994, Peter Shor showed that a quantum computer could factor large integers in polynomial time — a result that shocked the cryptographic world. The technical heart of his algorithm is a beautiful subroutine called Quantum Phase Estimation (QPE). It extracts the eigenvalue of a unitary operator with exponential precision, far beyond what classical sampling could achieve.

In this lab you will build QPE from scratch: understand the interference mechanism that writes a phase onto a register, simulate the inverse QFT that reads it out, recover the order of a number from the measured fraction, and finally see how noise erodes the algorithm's precision. No prior quantum mechanics required — just curiosity and a willingness to click buttons.

"Algorithms for quantum computation: discrete logarithms and factoring." — P. W. Shor, Proc. 35th Annual Symposium on Foundations of Computer Science, IEEE, 1994, pp. 124–134.

What you will build

  • Task 01 — Prepare the superposition and apply controlled-U gates; watch the phase accumulate on each ancilla qubit.
  • Task 02 — Apply the inverse QFT; watch the ancilla register collapse toward a single basis state.
  • Task 03 — Read the measurement outcome and run the continued-fraction algorithm to extract the order r.
  • Task 04 — Add phase noise; see how precision degrades and understand why more ancilla qubits help.

Background concepts

  • A phase θ encodes as e2πiθ — a rotation on the complex unit circle.
  • A unitary operator U has eigenstates |u⟩ with eigenvalue e2πiφ.
  • QPE uses n ancilla qubits to estimate φ to n binary digits of precision.
  • For Shor: U is modular multiplication U|y⟩ = |ay mod N⟩; its phase gives the order of a mod N.
  • From the order, the factors of N follow from gcd(ar/2 ± 1, N).

Task 01

Apply controlled-U gates and watch the phase accumulate.

QPE begins by putting n ancilla qubits into a uniform superposition with Hadamard gates. We then apply the gate U2k controlled on ancilla qubit k. Because the target register is in eigenstate |u⟩ of U with eigenvalue e2πiφ, this leaves the target unchanged but kicks the phase back onto the ancilla.

After Hadamard + controlled-U on ancilla k:
|qk⟩ → (|0⟩ + e2πi·2kφ |1⟩) / √2

Each ancilla encodes a different binary digit of φ. The full register, before the inverse QFT, is a tensor product of these single-qubit states. Step through each ancilla below to see the phase it accumulates.

Task 02

Apply the inverse QFT and see the register collapse to a single peak.

After phase kickback, the ancilla register encodes φ as a product state of single-qubit phases. The inverse Quantum Fourier Transform turns this distributed encoding into a sharp computational-basis peak. Constructive interference occurs near the integer k closest to φ · 2n; all other amplitudes cancel destructively.

This is the central miracle of QPE: the QFT converts a phase — something normally invisible to measurement — into a directly readable register value.

Ideal measurement distribution (n bits):
P(k) = |⟨k|IQFT|ψ⟩|² — peaked at k ≈ φ · 2n

Adjust the number of ancilla qubits and see how the histogram narrows. With more qubits, the peak sharpens and the estimated phase gets more decimal places of precision.

Task 03

Extract the order using continued fractions.

Measuring the QPE register gives an integer k. The ratio k / 2n is a rational approximation to s/r, where s is some integer and r is the order we want. To extract r, Shor uses the continued fraction expansion — a classical algorithm that finds the best rational approximation to any real number. The convergents of the expansion are candidates for s/r; we take the one whose denominator gives the smallest valid order.

For integer factoring, we seek the order r of a modulo N — meaning the smallest r such that ar ≡ 1 (mod N). Once we have r, the factors are gcd(ar/2 ± 1, N), with high probability.

Choose a base a and modulus N, set the number of ancilla qubits, and run QPE. The simulator will find the most likely measurement outcome and run the continued fraction algorithm on it. You will see every step of the fraction expansion and whether it leads to a valid factor.

Task 04

Add phase noise and watch the precision erode.

Real quantum hardware introduces phase noise — random rotations that blur each ancilla's phase. Concretely, each controlled-U gate applies an additional random phase error drawn from a Gaussian of width σ. The effect on the IQFT output is a broadening of the peak: small noise spreads probability across neighbouring states; large noise creates a nearly flat distribution.

More ancilla qubits means finer phase resolution — but also more gates, meaning more noise accumulates. There is a sweet spot where adding qubits still helps precision faster than noise destroys it. This interplay between circuit depth and noise is a central challenge of near-term quantum computing.

You built Shor's subroutine.

You have traced the full arc of Quantum Phase Estimation: phase kickback on ancilla qubits, constructive interference via the inverse QFT, continued-fraction readout to recover the order, and the degradation of precision under realistic phase noise.

Key result 1
Each ancilla qubit k accumulates the phase e2πi·2kφ. Together they encode φ in binary across the register.
Key result 2
The inverse QFT converts the distributed phase encoding into a measurement peak near k = φ·2n. More qubits → sharper peak → more precision.
Key result 3
Continued fractions extract a rational s/r from the measured k/2n. The denominator r is the multiplicative order, and gcd(ar/2±1, N) yields the factors.
Key result 4
Phase noise broadens the QPE peak. At low noise, adding ancilla qubits improves precision; at high noise, added circuit depth accumulates more error than the resolution gain is worth.

Shor's 1994 paper showed that this procedure runs in O((log N)3) quantum gate operations — polynomial in the number of digits of N. The best classical factoring algorithms (GNFS) run in sub-exponential but super-polynomial time. This asymptotic gap is the reason QPE is considered one of the most consequential algorithms ever discovered.
