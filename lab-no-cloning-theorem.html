Task 02

Can we build a quantum copy machine?

The obvious way to copy a qubit is a CNOT gate: use the qubit as the control and a fresh |0⟩ ancilla as the target. This works perfectly for computational-basis states — but entangles superpositions instead of cloning them.

Task 04

Interpret the no-cloning limit.

Task 01 showed that the Z-basis states and X-basis states are mutually unbiased: knowing the value in one basis gives you no information about the other. Task 02 showed that CNOT cannot clone a superposition — it produces an entangled Bell pair instead.

BB84 security is built on exactly this structural fact.

No-cloning theorem

There is no universal unitary operation U such that U |ψ⟩|0⟩ = |ψ⟩|ψ⟩ for every unknown state |ψ⟩.

In BB84, states from the Z and X bases are non-orthogonal as a set. Eve cannot perfectly copy an unknown incoming qubit and delay measurement until basis information is announced. Any attempt to extract information forces her to guess a basis — and wrong guesses disturb the state.

Intercept-resend is the best Eve can do without a quantum memory: measure immediately, resend a freshly prepared qubit in the measured basis. Half the time she guesses wrong, producing a state orthogonal to what Alice sent. That is the source of the detectable QBER signature you saw in Task 03.
