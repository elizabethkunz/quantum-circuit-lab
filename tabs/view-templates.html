The Bell pair

Two-qubit entanglement

H then CNOT yields |00⟩ + |11⟩ (normalized). The outcomes are always correlated — never |01⟩ or |10⟩ — even though neither qubit has a definite value alone. The experiment behind the 2022 physics Nobel.

CNOT, rebuilt from CZ

On many real hardware platforms (especially superconducting qubits), CZ is native and CNOT is emulated. The identity CNOT = (I⊗H)·CZ·(I⊗H) is how it's done. Same Bell state, different route — useful for understanding hardware-aware compilation.

Quantum teleportation

Moving state with entanglement

Prepare a state on q0, share a Bell pair between q1 and q2, then run the teleport protocol with deferred Pauli corrections. The state ends up on q2 while q0 and q1 become uniformly random byproducts. Nothing travels faster than light.

Deutsch's algorithm (balanced)

First quantum speedup

Determines whether a one-bit function is constant or balanced in a single query to the oracle — classically impossible. Here the oracle (a CNOT) implements a balanced function; running the circuit outputs 1 on q0 with certainty.

Superdense coding

Two bits in one qubit

Given a pre-shared Bell pair, Alice encodes two classical bits (here: 11) into a single qubit by applying one of {I, X, Z, XZ}, then sends it to Bob, who decodes. The dual of teleportation — one qubit of communication carries two classical bits.

Two wrongs should make a right

Two X gates in a row — algorithmically an identity. With zero noise, you get |0⟩ with certainty. Crank the noise and a "trivially correct" circuit silently turns into random guessing. The simplest possible illustration of error accumulation.
